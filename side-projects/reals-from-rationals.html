Constructing ℝ from ℚ

Two roads from the rationals to the reals — Cauchy sequences (Tao & Mathlib) and Dedekind cuts (Rudin) — with the mathematics beside the Lean, the way this repo formalizes it.

The rationals ℚ form an ordered field, yet they are riddled with holes. The classic witness is √2: no rational squares to 2, so x^2 = 2 has no solution in ℚ even though rationals approach the missing point arbitrarily closely. More structurally, ℚ fails to be complete in two equivalent senses — there are Cauchy sequences of rationals converging to nothing in ℚ (a metric gap), and nonempty bounded-above sets of rationals with no least upper bound (an order gap). The reals ℝ are the completion that plugs every such hole at once.

There are two canonical ways to build ℝ from ℚ, each filling one kind of gap directly. Cauchy sequences complete the metric: a real is the formal limit of a rational Cauchy sequence, with two sequences identified when their difference tends to zero. This is Tao's route (Analysis I, Ch. 5), what the teorth/analysis companion formalizes, and exactly how Mathlib defines ℝ. Dedekind cuts fill the order gaps: a real is the downward-closed set of all rationals to its left, and completeness falls out almost for free because the supremum of a family of cuts is just their union.

What is the best approach?

Use Cauchy sequences as the default — and that is what this repo and Mathlib actually do. The decisive advantage is that arithmetic lifts mechanically from ℚ: +, \times, -, {}^{-1} are defined termwise on representative sequences and pushed through the quotient, so the ring/field axioms reduce to routine ε-arguments on rationals. Mathlib's ℝ is a one-field wrapper around CauSeq.Completion.Cauchy abs — the quotient of rational Cauchy sequences by "difference → 0" — and the companion mirrors it with abbrev Real := Quotient CauchySequence.instSetoid. The same machinery also reuses verbatim for other completions (e.g. the p-adics), and it is the form in which limits and continuity are most natural.

Reach for Dedekind cuts when you want order intuition and the least-upper-bound property almost for free: the sup of a bounded-above family of cuts is their union, and completeness is a three-line check. The price is paid on the algebra side — the additive inverse needs a slack term, and multiplication needs a four-case sign split — which is exactly why Mathlib does not use cuts. (Classically the two constructions are isomorphic; constructively they differ. Mathlib is classical, so its choice of Cauchy is convenience, not necessity.)

Bottom line for this repo: Cauchy is the primary, formalized construction; Dedekind cuts are the illuminating alternative for order and completeness.

1 · Cauchy sequences

Tao builds ℝ in three stages. First (5.1) he isolates rational Cauchy sequences via eventual ε-steadiness, where ε ranges over the positive rationals only — ℝ does not yet exist, so real ε would be circular. Next (5.2) he says when two Cauchy sequences are equivalent (eventually ε-close for every rational ε > 0). Finally (5.3) ℝ is the set of formal limits \mathrm{LIM}\,a_n — equivalence classes — with +, ×, negation and reciprocals checked to be well-defined on classes.

The companion follows this exactly, with one Lean twist: a "sequence of rationals" is not a bare \mathbb{N}\to\mathbb{Q} but a Sequence structure with an integer start index. Note too that this is a teaching snapshot — several supporting results are still sorry (the setoid laws, multiplication's closure, the field axioms); addition's well-definedness is fully proven. The construction's shape is complete and faithful.

2 · Dedekind cuts

Where Cauchy sequences complete the metric, cuts fill the order gaps directly: a real number is the set of all rationals to its left. We follow Rudin's one-set formulation (Principles of Mathematical Analysis, Ch. 1 Appendix). A cut \alpha\subseteq\mathbb{Q} is nonempty and proper, downward-closed, and has no greatest element; ℝ is the set of all cuts, ordered by inclusion. The payoff is completeness for almost nothing. The bill comes due on algebra — the additive inverse needs a slack term and multiplication needs a sign-case split — which is exactly the asymmetry that makes Mathlib prefer Cauchy.

Note: the Lean below is illustrative and is not part of Mathlib or the companion. Mathlib builds ℝ from Cauchy sequences; its only Dedekind* names (IsDedekindDomain, …) are commutative-algebra notions, unrelated to order-theoretic cuts. The structure below is hand-written, type-checked by static analysis, to show how a cut-based ℝ would look in idiomatic Lean 4.

4 · The same √2, two ways

√2 is the cleanest witness to the holes in ℚ, and it surfaces in both constructions.

Cauchy view

Take any rational sequence converging to √2 — the Heron iteration a_0=1,\ a_{n+1}=\tfrac{a_n}{2}+\tfrac{1}{a_n}, giving 1,\tfrac{3}{2},\tfrac{17}{12},\tfrac{577}{408},\dots, or simply the decimal truncations 1,1.4,1.41,1.414,\dots. Each is a perfectly good rational Cauchy sequence, yet has no limit inside ℚ. The real √2 is, by definition, the class \mathrm{LIM}\,a_n; any other sequence squeezing toward the same point is identified with it (their termwise difference → 0). In the companion this is LIM a; in Mathlib, Real.mk of the corresponding CauSeq ℚ abs.
